HR Analytics Dashboard

Visualizing and analyzing key workforce metrics

Project Overview

This project focuses on developing an interactive HR Analytics Dashboard in Excel to help HR managers analyze key workforce metrics such as employee performance, attendance, attrition, and salary trends.
The goal was to:

  • Transform raw HR data into a dynamic, visual dashboard.
  • Provide insights on employee engagement and productivity.
  • Identify performance patterns across departments.
  • Support data-driven workforce management and planning.
  • Enable quick and informed decision-making.



The DataSet

The dataset consisted of 100 employee records with fields such as:

  • Employee ID, Gender, Age, Department, Education Level
  • Salary, Attendance %, Overtime Hours
  • Performance Rating, Promotion Status, Attrition Status
The data provided a well-rounded view of HR-related factors, enabling analysis across multiple employee dimensions.



Methodology

1. Data Cleaning

  • Checked for duplicates, missing values, and incorrect data types.
  • Standardized formats for dates, salaries, and attendance percentages.
  • Used IF functions to create new analytical columns:
    • Performance Evaluation – classified employees based on performance rating.
    • Most Leaves Taken – identified employees with frequent leaves.
    • Most Overtime Worked – flagged employees exceeding average overtime hours.
  • Ensured data consistency for use in pivot tables and charts.

2. Visualization in Excel

Pivot tables, pivot charts and advanced charts were created in Excel to examine workforce distribution, attendance patterns, compensation structure and deeper efficiency signals across departments.

Pivot Tables

Pivot tables were used to summarize and analyze key HR metrics.

1. Employee Count by Department

Shows the workforce distribution: HR: 27, IT: 23, Marketing: 22, Sales: 17, Finance: 11.
Why it matters: headcount size influences capacity, training needs and per-department overhead; large teams (HR) can be sources of scalable processes.

2. Department Efficiency Heatmap

Highlights Average Attendance, Performance Rating, and Overtime Hours per department.

  • HR: high attendance (86.8%) and highest performance (3.04).
  • IT: decent attendance but lowest performance (2.65) - a pay-performance gap signal.
  • Marketing: high overtime (~16.9 hrs) but lowest attendance (80.8%) - classic burnout signal.

Note: heatmap colors were used to prioritize which departments require diagnostic follow-ups (training, workload audits, or retention actions).

3. Salary–Attendance Correlation

Compares average monthly salary vs attendance by department. Finance and IT pay ~$80k+, while Sales maintains top attendance at a mid-range salary.
Interpretation: compensation alone does not guarantee engagement - helpful for pay-for-performance planning.

4. Gender Distribution

Workforce diversity: 41 female, 40 male, 19 other.
Why it matters: balanced representation supports equitable talent programs and reduces bias in retention/compensation analysis.

5. Department That Earns the Most

Average monthly salary by department: IT: $81,561, Finance: $80,287, Marketing: $69,912.
Actionable insight: investigate pay–performance alignment in IT to improve ROI on compensation.

Deep Insights, Interpretation & Business Impact

This section translates dashboard visuals into actionable business insights that go beyond descriptive reporting - identifying inefficiencies, forecasting risks, and recommending measurable HR interventions.

1. Pay is not equal to Performance in IT

What the charts show

IT has the highest average salary (~$81,561) but the lowest average performance rating (~2.65).

Interpretation

Investment in IT compensation is not translating into stronger output. This points to possible hiring mismatches, skill gaps, or ineffective training programs.

Business Impact

Overpaying underperforming teams inflates labor cost without corresponding productivity. This reduces ROI per headcount and can distort annual budgeting accuracy.

Recommended Actions

  • Conduct a skill-gap audit and identify top 3 missing technical skills within 2 weeks.
  • Launch a 90-day Performance Improvement Program (PIP) for low-performers; track rating improvement post-training.

Metric to track: Salary-to-performance ratio by department (monthly).

2. Marketing: Low Attendance + High Overtime = Burnout Risk

What the charts show

Marketing has the lowest attendance (~80.8%) yet among the highest average overtime (~16.95 hrs).

Interpretation

Employees appear overworked but disengaged - an indicator of uneven workload distribution or fatigue-driven absenteeism. Sustained imbalance can erode creativity and delivery quality.

Business Impact

Burnout inflates turnover costs, delays marketing campaigns, and weakens team morale - reducing campaign ROI and increasing rehiring frequency.

Recommended Actions

  • Run a shift & task analysis to redistribute or automate repetitive workloads.
  • Introduce flex days or a quarterly “no-overtime week” and measure the effect on attendance & output.

Metric to track: Correlation of overtime vs absenteeism (R coefficient) and monthly attrition within Marketing.

3. Sales: High Attendance but Mid-Level Pay means Retention Vulnerability

What the charts show

Sales maintains the highest attendance (~89%) yet only earns a mid-range salary (~$71,225).

Interpretation

Sales staff demonstrate loyalty and engagement; however, stagnant pay could make them susceptible to external offers. Retention risk is elevated for top performers, especially if incentive structures change.

Business Impact

Losing top-performing sales reps directly reduces revenue and disrupts client relationships, while replacement costs remain high due to retraining time.

Recommended Actions

  • Run a top-performer retention analysis (identify top 10% by revenue contribution & assess pay competitiveness).
  • Design a targeted retention package (bonuses + recognition programs) and A/B test outcomes over one quarter.

Metric to track: Voluntary attrition among top 20% sales performers & quota attainment pre/post retention strategy.

4. Overtime Has Little Positive Effect on Performance

What the charts show

The scatter plot reveals almost zero correlation (R² ~ 0.01) between overtime hours and performance.

Interpretation

More hours do not equate to better output - suggesting that overtime could represent inefficient task management or diminishing productivity returns.

Business Impact

Paying overtime without performance gain wastes resources and signals potential workflow inefficiencies.

Recommended Actions

  • Replace hours-based metrics with outcome-driven KPIs tied to measurable deliverables.
  • Initiate process improvement projects to identify top 10 repetitive tasks causing excessive OT hours.

Metric to track: Overtime cost per unit of output (e.g., per deliverable or task).

5. HR: High Performance + Large Team = Scale Best Practices

What the charts show

HR has the highest headcount (27) and a solid average performance rating (~3.04).

Interpretation & Opportunity

HR’s strong results suggest effective internal systems (training, onboarding, knowledge sharing). These methods can be scaled across underperforming departments.

Business Impact

Standardizing HR’s effective practices could uplift organizational performance faster than department-specific interventions - improving efficiency without additional headcount.

Recommended Actions

  • Document HR’s high-impact processes and pilot them in IT or Marketing departments.
  • Measure post-transfer performance improvement versus control group to validate ROI.
