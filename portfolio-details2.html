Job Market Analysis

Data Cleaning & Tableau visualizations on the Canadian Job Bank Dataset

Project Overview

This project analyzes the Canadian Job Market using the Job Bank Dataset (July 2025)
The goal was to uncover insights on:

  • Highest-paying job roles across Canada
  • Vacancy trends by province and job title
  • Salary vs. demand (experience level analysis)
  • Finally, deliver an interactive tool that helps in decision-making



The DataSet

This project uses the Canadian Job Bank Dataset (July 2025), obtained from the official Government of Canada Job Bank website. It contains job postings with details such as job titles, vacancies, provinces, and salary ranges. The data was cleaned in Excel before creating Tableau visualizations.



Methodology

1. Data Cleaning

Performed data cleaning and preparation in Excel to make the dataset accurate and consistent for visualization in Tableau.

  1. Removed unnecessary columns to keep only relevant fields (Job Title, Province, Vacancies, Salaries, Experience Level).
  2. Standardized job titles by cleaning duplicates, fixing spellings, and merging similar roles.
  3. Corrected and formatted the Date column for consistency.
  4. Handled duplicates and removed records with missing job titles.
  5. Replaced NA values with Not Specified in the Salary fields.
  6. Created a calculated Average Salary column from Min and Max salary values.
  7. Adjusted unrealistic or zero salary values (e.g., less than $15/hour) for accuracy.
  8. Derived a clean Annual Salary column using formulas and SWITCH logic.
  9. Standardized the Experience Level column into consistent ranges (e.g., "1–3 years").

2. Visualization in Tableau

Developed a suite of Tableau dashboards to answer real-world questions about the Canadian job market. Each chart was designed to solve a specific problem faced by job seekers, policymakers, and employers, making the insights both actionable and easy to explore.

Key Dashboards & Charts

  • “Where Should I Work” Tool:
    Many job seekers wonder, “Given my skills and priorities, what jobs should I actually apply for?”
    This interactive tool allows users to select their province, experience level, and ranking preference (Salary vs Vacancy). They can even type in the exact number of jobs (Top N) they want to see. KPIs instantly update to show either:
    • Highest Salary Job Title and Highest Salary (if ranked by Salary)
    • Top In-Demand Job and Total Vacancies (if ranked by Vacancy)
    This makes the dashboard feel like a career decision-support tool rather than just a set of charts.
  • Vacancies by Province & Job Roles:
    Companies may offer good salaries, but “Which jobs actually have the most openings?”
    This dashboard highlights the roles with the highest number of vacancies in each province, solving the problem of matching job seekers with real opportunities in their preferred region.
  • Pay vs Demand (Dual-Axis):
    Candidates often face a trade-off: “Should I focus on salary, or jobs in higher demand?”
    This dual-axis chart plots experience levels against vacancy counts and average annual salaries. It reveals how demand and pay shift across experience ranges—guiding users on whether to prioritize entry-level accessibility or long-term earning potential.
  • Job Market Heatmap:
    Job markets differ drastically across Canada. “Which jobs are most in demand in each province, and how well do they pay?”
    The heatmap answers this by showing the top 5 in-demand jobs per province. Square size represents vacancies, while darker color shades indicate higher salaries.
  • Top 10 Salaries by Province:
    Job seekers often ask, “Which roles are the highest-paying in my province?”
    This chart ranks the top 10 jobs by average annual salary, with a province filter for side-by-side comparison. It helps users quickly identify where the best-paying opportunities are located.

Key Insights & Findings

  • Salary vs Demand Trade-Off: Entry-level roles tend to have higher vacancy counts but lower salaries, while senior roles offer better pay but fewer openings. This highlights the challenge job seekers face when deciding between quick entry and long-term earning potential.
  • Provincial Job Market Gaps: Certain provinces like Ontario and British Columbia show the highest demand for jobs, while others offer competitive pay but fewer opportunities. This helps job seekers decide whether to stay local or relocate.
  • Top-Paying Roles Identified: Specialized technical and healthcare positions consistently rank among the highest-paying jobs across provinces, giving job seekers clear targets for career development.
  • In-Demand Occupations: Retail, customer service, and IT support roles dominate vacancy counts, especially for early-career professionals. This indicates areas where demand is unlikely to drop in the near term.
  • Decision-Support Tool for Job Seekers: The “Where Should I Work” dashboard empowers users to filter by province, experience level, and ranking preference (Salary vs Vacancy), transforming raw data into a practical career-planning guide.

Business Impact

This project goes beyond data visualization—it delivers actionable insights for multiple stakeholders:

  • For Job Seekers: Empowers individuals to identify high-paying roles, understand demand by province, and make informed career choices based on salary vs vacancy trade-offs.
  • For Employers: Helps companies benchmark salaries, identify competitive provinces for hiring, and anticipate workforce supply-demand imbalances.
  • For Policymakers: Provides visibility into labor market gaps, enabling more targeted education, training, and immigration policies to address skill shortages.

By transforming raw labor market data into interactive dashboards, this project acts as a decision-support system-turning complex datasets into practical guidance for real-world career and workforce planning.
